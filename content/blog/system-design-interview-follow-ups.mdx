Candidates obsess over the opening prompt — *"Design a URL shortener"* — and under-prepare for the part that actually decides the interview: the **follow-ups**. The interviewer's job isn't to see if you can draw a box labeled "server." It's to push on that drawing until it breaks, and watch how you respond.

The good news: the pushes are predictable. There are about five of them, and once you can name them, you can prepare answers in advance.

## Where every design starts

You'll almost always begin here — one server, one database. This is correct, and you should *say* it's correct: "I'd start simple and scale when the numbers demand it."

Then the pushing starts.

## Follow-up 1 — "Now it gets 100× the traffic"

This is the scaling probe. The instinct to resist is "make the server bigger" (vertical scaling) — say it has a ceiling, then go **horizontal**: multiple stateless app servers behind a **load balancer**.

The word that earns points here is **stateless**. If a server stores session state in memory, you can't freely route requests across the fleet. Push state into a shared store (a cache or the DB) so any server can handle any request. This is the core idea in [Load Balancing](/learn/intermediate/load-balancing).

## Follow-up 2 — "The database is the bottleneck now"

You scaled the app tier; the single database is now the choke point. Walk the standard ladder, cheapest first:

1. **Cache the hot reads.** Put a cache in front (see [Caching Strategies](/learn/intermediate/caching-strategies)). Most reads never hit the DB. Mention the failure mode — the [thundering herd](/blog/thundering-herd) — to show depth.
2. **Read replicas.** Send reads to replicas, writes to the primary. Name the catch yourself: [replication lag](/blog/read-replicas-lie) and stale reads.
3. **Shard** when writes outgrow one machine. Talk about choosing a shard key and the risk of hot partitions ([Sharding](/learn/advanced/database-sharding)).

## Follow-up 3 — "That component just died. What happens?"

The availability probe. For **every** box you've drawn, the interviewer can ask "what if this one dies?" Have an answer ready for each:

- App server dies → load balancer health-checks it out; traffic reroutes. No impact.
- Cache dies → reads fall through to the DB (slower, but up). Watch the herd.
- Primary DB dies → promote a replica (failover); note the writes that hadn't replicated may be lost.

The phrase to reach for is **single point of failure**. Scan your own diagram for any box with no backup and call it out *before* they do. That's the move that signals seniority.

<Callout type="tip" title="Interview tell">
Junior answers add components. Senior answers also say how each one *fails* and what the blast radius is. Volunteer the failure modes — don't wait to be asked.
</Callout>

## Follow-up 4 — "This part is slow / spiky. Can we make it async?"

Not everything needs to happen inside the request. Sending the welcome email, transcoding the video, updating analytics — push these onto a **queue** and let workers handle them. The user gets a fast response; the slow work happens in the background, and a traffic spike fills the queue instead of toppling the app ([Message Queues](/learn/intermediate/message-queues)).

The senior caveat: queues are **at-least-once**, so consumers must be [idempotent](/blog/idempotency-saves-payments). Saying that unprompted is worth a lot.

## Follow-up 5 — "What are the trade-offs of what you just proposed?"

The meta-question, and the one that separates levels. There is no perfect design — only trade-offs. When you add a cache, *say* "now I have a consistency problem." When you add replicas, *say* "now I have lag." When you shard, *say* "now cross-shard queries are painful."

This is [CAP](/learn/advanced/cap-theorem) thinking applied to your whole design: every box you add buys something and costs something. Naming both is the entire game.

## How to practice

Don't practice *designs* — practice *follow-ups*. Take any prompt, sketch the simple version, then run all five pushes against it yourself: 100× traffic, DB bottleneck, a dead component, an async opportunity, the trade-offs. The [expert design walkthroughs](/learn/expert/design-url-shortener) are built exactly this way — start simple, then apply pressure.

## The takeaway

The opening prompt is the easy part. The interview lives in the follow-ups, and they're the same five almost every time: **scale the traffic, scale the data, survive a failure, go async, name the trade-offs.** Prepare those, narrate your reasoning out loud, and volunteer the weaknesses in your own design — that's what a strong signal looks like.
