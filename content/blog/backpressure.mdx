The queue was supposed to be the safety net. Orders came in fast, workers processed them in the background, everything was decoupled and resilient. Then marketing ran a flash sale.

Orders arrived at 50,000/sec. Workers drained 5,000/sec. The queue did exactly what a queue does: it buffered the difference. For about four minutes. Then the broker ran out of memory and the whole pipeline stopped — including the orders that were already safely enqueued.

This is the failure mode nobody draws on the whiteboard: **the queue absorbed the spike right up until it couldn't, and then it failed worse than no queue at all.**

## The arithmetic is unforgiving

A queue only "decouples" producers and consumers when, *over time*, consumers keep up. The queue smooths bursts; it does not create throughput. If the **sustained** inflow exceeds the **sustained** drain rate, the backlog grows linearly forever:

Two numbers to watch (and alert on): **queue depth** and **oldest-message age**. Depth tells you the backlog size; age tells you how stale the work is — a depth of 10M with a rising age means a customer's "order confirmed" email is now arriving an hour late, if at all.

<Callout type="warn" title="An unbounded queue is a hidden memory leak">
A queue with no size limit doesn't prevent overload — it just delays it and converts it into an out-of-memory crash. The most dangerous queue is the one configured to grow 'as needed,' because 'as needed' under a spike means 'until the broker dies.'
</Callout>

## Backpressure is the queue pushing back

**Backpressure** is the mechanism by which a slow consumer (or a full queue) signals "slow down" back upstream, so the system degrades gracefully instead of collapsing. You have four levers, roughly in order of preference.

### 1. Add consumers (scale the drain)

The first and most obvious: if inflow is 50k/sec, you need enough workers to drain ~50k/sec. Autoscale the consumer pool on **queue depth**, not CPU — CPU looks fine while the backlog explodes. Because each unit of work is independent, adding workers scales the drain linearly (this is the whole shape of [Message Queues](/learn/intermediate/message-queues)).

### 2. Bound the queue, then shed load

Give the queue a hard limit. When it's full, you must *choose* what to sacrifice — and choosing is better than crashing:

- **Reject** new work (return `429`/`503` to producers — and have them [back off](/blog/the-429-nobody-plans-for)).
- **Drop** the lowest-value messages (sample analytics events; you don't need all of them).
- **Shed** to a dead-letter queue for later, off the hot path.

```python
# bounded queue: refuse work instead of growing without limit
if queue.depth() >= MAX_DEPTH:
    raise TooBusy(retry_after=5)   # backpressure: tell the producer to wait
queue.put(message)
```

### 3. Rate-limit the producers

Sometimes the right answer is to not accept the firehose at all. A token bucket at ingress (see [Rate Limiting](/learn/intermediate/rate-limiting)) caps inflow to what the system can actually sustain, turning a cliff into a controlled queue.

### 4. Prioritize

Not all work is equal. A separate high-priority lane for "process payment" vs. "update recommendation model" means the important work drains even while the backlog of nice-to-haves grows.

<Callout type="tip" title="Decoupled doesn't mean infinite">
The mental model that bites people: 'we have a queue, so producers and consumers are independent.' They're independent *in the short term*. Over any sustained window, consumers set the ceiling. Size the consumer fleet for sustained peak, and bound the queue for the burst on top.
</Callout>

## The takeaway

A queue buys you a buffer for *bursts*, not a license to ignore throughput. If consumers can't sustain the inflow, the queue doesn't save you — it just changes how you fail, from "drop some requests" to "crash everything at once." Bound the buffer, scale the drain on depth, and build a backpressure signal so the system can say "slow down" before it has to say nothing at all.
