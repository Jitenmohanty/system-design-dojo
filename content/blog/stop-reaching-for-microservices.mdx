There's a moment on most young teams where someone says: *"We should split this into microservices before it becomes a big ball of mud."* It sounds responsible. It's usually a mistake.

Here's the uncomfortable thesis: **microservices are a solution to an organizational problem, not a technical one.** And if you adopt them before you have that problem, you don't get the benefits — you just import the costs.

## What microservices actually buy you

The real win of microservices isn't performance or "scalability" in the abstract. It's **independent teams shipping independently.** When you have 200 engineers, a single codebase becomes a coordination nightmare: merge conflicts, one team's bug blocking everyone's deploy, a release train nobody can board.

Splitting into services with hard boundaries lets Team A deploy without asking Team B. That autonomy is the prize. The defining rule — covered in [Microservices](/learn/advanced/microservices) — is **database-per-service**: no shared tables, so teams can't secretly couple through the data layer.

<Callout type="info" title="The tell">
Microservices are an answer to 'too many people in one codebase.' If you don't have too many people, you don't have the question.
</Callout>

## What you actually build with three engineers

Three engineers don't have a coordination problem. But split prematurely and here's what you get — the **distributed monolith**: services that *look* independent but are wired together so tightly they must be deployed in lockstep.

You now own *every* hard problem from the [Distributed Systems](/learn/advanced/distributed-systems) lesson — network failures between your own components, partial failures, distributed transactions, eventual consistency, tracing a request across five hops — and in exchange you got **zero** team autonomy, because the shared database means you still deploy everything together.

That's the worst square on the grid: maximum operational pain, minimum benefit.

## The costs you signed up for without noticing

- **A function call became a network call.** In-process, it's nanoseconds and can't fail. Across services it's milliseconds, and it can time out, retry, and double-fire (hello, [idempotency](/blog/idempotency-saves-payments)).
- **A stack trace became a detective case.** One user action spans five services and three queues. You now *need* distributed tracing just to answer "why was this slow?"
- **A transaction became a saga.** `BEGIN; ...; COMMIT;` across two services doesn't exist. You hand-roll compensating actions and live with windows of inconsistency.
- **Local dev needs Docker Compose and a prayer.** "Run the app" now means running eight things.

## What to do instead: the modular monolith

Start with **one deployable**, but keep clean module boundaries *inside* it — separate packages, a rule that modules talk through defined interfaces, ideally separate schemas in the same database. You get most of the conceptual tidiness of services with none of the network.

```text
monolith/
  modules/
    users/      ← owns user tables, exposes a UsersService interface
    orders/     ← calls usersService.getName() — an in-process call, not HTTP
    billing/
  main.ts       ← one deploy, one log stream, one debugger
```

When a module genuinely needs to scale or ship on its own cadence — and you have a team to own it — *that* is when you extract it into a service. The boundaries are already there, so extraction is mechanical instead of archaeological.

<Callout type="tip" title="The rule of thumb">
Don't split by service until you're splitting by *team*. Conway's Law cuts both ways: design the architecture to match the org you have, not the org you imagine.
</Callout>

## The takeaway

Microservices are a fine answer to "too many people, one codebase." They are a terrible answer to "our code is getting messy" — for that, the answer is *boundaries*, and you can have boundaries in a monolith. Earn the complexity before you pay for it.
