Sharding promised to spread the load. You split the database across eight shards, watched the dashboards balance out, and moved on.

Then a celebrity with 80 million followers posted, and shard 3 — the one holding their account — went to 100% CPU while shards 0, 1, 2, and 4–7 sat at 5%. Adding more shards didn't help, because the problem wasn't *capacity*. It was that one key was doing all the work.

This is a **hot partition** (or hot shard), and it's the failure mode that turns "we scaled horizontally" into "we have eight databases and one of them is on fire."

## Even sharding isn't even

Sharding splits data across nodes by a **shard key** — covered in [Database Sharding](/learn/advanced/database-sharding). The unspoken assumption is that load spreads evenly across keys. Real-world data refuses to cooperate: access follows power laws. A few users, products, or tenants get orders of magnitude more traffic than the rest.

If your shard key concentrates that skew onto one node, that node becomes the bottleneck for the entire system:

The cruel part: your *average* shard utilization looks healthy (maybe 15%), so capacity dashboards say everything's fine. The system is down anyway, because one shard is saturated. **Averages hide hotspots** — you have to watch per-shard tail metrics, not the mean.

<Callout type="danger" title="More shards won't save a hot key">
The instinct under load is 'add shards.' But a single hot key lives on exactly one shard no matter how many you have. Resharding from 8 to 16 just gives the hot key a roomier mansion to overload alone. You have to break up the key's load, not add neighbors.
</Callout>

## Fix 1 — Pick a key that doesn't concentrate

The cheapest fix is upstream: choose a shard key with naturally high cardinality and even access. Sharding tweets by `user_id` concentrates a celebrity's entire firehose on one shard; sharding by `tweet_id` spreads that same user's tweets across all of them. The right key depends on your hottest query — but "what's the highest-traffic entity, and does this key isolate it?" is the question to ask *before* you shard.

## Fix 2 — Salt the hot key

When one key is unavoidably hot, **split it** by appending a small random suffix, turning one logical key into N physical sub-keys on different shards:

```text
# one hot key → one melting shard
counter:celebrity_likes

# salted into 10 sub-keys → spread across shards, summed on read
counter:celebrity_likes:0
counter:celebrity_likes:1
...
counter:celebrity_likes:9
```

Writes pick a random suffix (spreading the load); reads fan out to all ten and aggregate. You trade a slightly more expensive read for not having a single shard absorb 100% of the writes. This is the standard fix for hot counters and write-heavy hot keys.

## Fix 3 — Put a cache in front of the hot read

If the hotness is **reads** (everyone fetching the same celebrity profile), a cache absorbs it before it ever reaches the shard. The hot key becomes the *easiest* thing to serve — one cached value answering millions of reads — instead of the hardest. Just respect the [thundering herd](/blog/thundering-herd): when that hot key expires, coalesce the reload so you don't slam the melting shard with 10,000 simultaneous misses.

## Fix 4 — Spread placement with consistent hashing + vnodes

Even without a single super-key, plain `hash(key) % N` can clump ranges unevenly and makes every reshard a mass-migration. **Consistent hashing with virtual nodes** spreads each physical node's ownership across many small arcs of the ring, smoothing lumps and limiting how much data moves when you add or remove a node. That's the entire point of [Consistent Hashing](/learn/advanced/consistent-hashing).

<Callout type="tip" title="Alert on the max, not the mean">
Set your capacity alerts on the *hottest* shard's CPU and the *busiest* key's request rate — not cluster averages. A hot partition is invisible to mean-based dashboards right up until it takes the system down.
</Callout>

## The takeaway

Horizontal scaling assumes load spreads evenly, and real data never does — access concentrates on a few hot entities. Sharding distributes *capacity*, but a bad shard key re-concentrates *load* onto one node, and no number of extra shards fixes a single hot key. Choose keys that isolate your hottest entity, salt unavoidable hot keys, cache hot reads, and watch per-shard maxes instead of averages.
